# Fig & Bloom Blog Thought-Leader Lens Workflow

Use this workflow for every Fig & Bloom blog post: new drafts, rewrites, Shopify draft updates, SEO refreshes, and editorial briefs.

## Core stack

Default lenses:

1. **Chet Holmes — editorial strategy**
   - Make the post genuinely useful for the 97% who are not buying today.
   - Lead with education and decision help, not the pitch.

2. **Eugene Schwartz — reader awareness**
   - Match the headline/opening to the reader’s actual state: product-aware, problem-aware, feeling-aware, or lifestyle/unaware.
   - Channel existing desire; do not manufacture desire.

3. **Seth Godin — identity / Moment Maker lens**
   - Connect to who the reader wants to be: someone who marks moments beautifully and intentionally.
   - Use “people like us do things like this” as the identity engine behind the post.

4. **Zig Ziglar — warmth and sincerity**
   - Make the language human, plain-spoken, relationship-led, and emotionally safe.
   - Remove cleverness that gets in the way.

5. **David Ogilvy — quality control**
   - Add specificity. Protect the brand image. Avoid generic florist/lifestyle fluff.
   - Use real Fig & Bloom products, flowers, colours, delivery realities, and design rationale.

## Specialist overlays

Use only where appropriate:

- **Tony Robbins:** state change / future memory. Best for get well, grief, recovery, celebration, self/home atmosphere.
- **Robert Cialdini:** proof, authority, belonging. Best for review-led, florist craft, customer story, trust, and “what people do” posts.
- **Alex Hormozi:** offer architecture. Best for bundles, subscriptions, guarantees, conversion sections.
- **Gary Halbert:** visceral urgent moments. Best for ad hooks or urgent occasion landing pages; do not let it dominate the blog voice.
- **Joe Girard:** relationship systems. Best for reminder flows, follow-up, corporate/client relationship posts.

## Procedure for every blog post

### 1. Identify the moment

Define:
- occasion/context,
- sender/reader role,
- recipient emotional state,
- what the sender is afraid of getting wrong,
- what a good outcome feels like.

### 2. Pick reader awareness using Schwartz

Choose one:
- **Product-aware:** already searching for flowers/delivery/specific occasion.
- **Problem-aware:** knows the situation but not the right gesture.
- **Feeling-aware:** feels guilt, care, gratitude, love, awkwardness, uncertainty.
- **Lifestyle/unaware:** wants beauty, ritual, hosting, or identity reinforcement.

This determines headline, opening, and CTA.

### 3. Make it useful with Holmes

The post must reduce one real anxiety or teach one practical decision. If it cannot answer “what should I do now?”, it is not ready.

### 4. Add the Moment Maker identity with Godin

Quietly reinforce: people like us notice, remember, host, thank, apologise, comfort, or celebrate with intention.

### 5. Humanise with Ziglar

Use sincere lines that sound like a thoughtful friend, not a marketer. Especially important for grief, apology, get well, new mum, thank you, and support moments.

### 6. Specificity pass with Ogilvy

Add concrete Fig & Bloom detail:
- named bouquets where relevant,
- colour palette / flower composition,
- delivery reality,
- vase/card/add-on pathway,
- florist craft or design rationale,
- no vague “premium beautiful bouquet” filler.

### 7. Choose CTA by intent

- Product-aware → shop / choose delivery date / browse relevant bouquet.
- Problem-aware → recommendation path / occasion guide / card wording.
- Feeling-aware → send the gesture / mark the moment.
- Lifestyle/unaware → save the guide / join list / explore ritual or hosting category.

## Required QA before publication

- Does the post help before it sells?
- Does the headline match awareness state?
- Does it feel Fig & Bloom, not generic florist SEO?
- Are florals/products real and specific where mentioned?
- Is the tone warm, intentional, Australian English, non-salesy?
- Is there a clear next action?
- Does the post protect the brand image?

## Guardrails

- Do not use all lenses visibly in the copy. Use them as editorial machinery behind the piece.
- Do not invent product details, bouquets, editorial plans, or campaign facts. Use real Fig & Bloom products and real Paperclip/Notion source content.
- Blog posts should feel useful, tasteful, human, and commercially purposeful — never like a framework dump.
