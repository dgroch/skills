# Fig & Bloom — Visual Rubric

This rubric defines the brand's visual standards for evaluating photography
and UGC. It is consumed by the `photography-ugc-review` skill as part of
the brand config input.

Calibrated from 15 approved reference images and the Fig & Bloom social
feed guidelines document.

---

## Brand Essence

Warm, genuine, real-life moments with flowers. Aspirational but accessible
— not luxury-magazine stiff. The feeling is: someone received beautiful
flowers and wanted to capture the moment. Understated, quietly confident,
never performative.

---

## Evaluation Dimensions

### 1. Lighting

**Standard:** Natural light. Flower colours must read as true-to-life.

- APPROVE: Natural daylight (bright or overcast), soft indoor ambient
  light, warm window light. No harsh shadows on faces or petals.
- COMMENT: Slightly underexposed or slightly cool-toned, but flower
  colours are still readable. Could be corrected in post.
- REJECT: Flash photography, harsh artificial light, heavy shadows that
  obscure the bouquet, blown-out whites where petal detail is lost.

**Notes:**
- Cool-toned natural light is acceptable — not every image needs warmth.
- Overcast outdoor light is fine.
- The key test: can you accurately see the flower colours? If yes, lighting
  passes.

### 2. Composition

**Standard:** The bouquet is the hero or co-hero. Never secondary or
obscured.

- APPROVE: Bouquet occupies significant visual real estate. Background is
  clean and uncluttered. Framing gives the flowers presence. Person +
  bouquet, product-only, or behind-the-scenes formats all accepted.
- COMMENT: Bouquet is present but competing with a busy background or
  unclear focal hierarchy.
- REJECT: Bouquet is small, obscured, or clearly secondary to the person
  or setting. Cluttered or distracting backgrounds that pull attention
  from the flowers.

**Accepted formats:**
- Person holding bouquet (full-body, three-quarter, close-crop)
- Product-only (bouquet on surface, styled or casual)
- Mirror selfie with bouquet
- Behind-the-scenes / phone-in-frame shots
- Close-up of bouquet detail

**Notes:**
- Phone camera grid lines visible in frame = still acceptable.
- Instagram UI elements or text overlays = acceptable (assess the
  photography underneath).

### 3. Colour Grading

**Standard:** No heavy filters. Flower colours appear true-to-life.

- APPROVE: Natural, unfiltered colour or very light editing. Palette reads
  as authentic — pinks look like pinks, whites look like whites, greens
  look like greens.
- COMMENT: Light filter applied but flower colours are still recognisable.
  Mild warmth or contrast adjustment that doesn't distort.
- REJECT: Heavy filters that distort flower colours (VSCO-heavy, high
  contrast, desaturated, sepia, aggressive colour grading). Flowers look
  unnatural.

**Brand palette tendencies (for reference, not strict enforcement):**
- Soft pinks, whites, creams, blush tones
- Sage greens, eucalyptus grey-greens
- Vivid magenta/fuchsia as accent (actual flower colour, not a filter)
- Deep reds for roses
- Native textures: banksia golds, protea pinks, dried straw tones
- Wrapping: white tissue, black satin ribbon

**Notes:**
- The palette above describes what's common, not what's mandatory. The
  real test is whether colours look true-to-life, not whether they match
  a specific hex value.
- Background tones should lean neutral — white walls, concrete, natural
  textures, greenery. But this is compositional context, not a colour
  gate.

### 4. Styling & Art Direction

**Standard:** Bouquet is well-presented. Fig & Bloom packaging visible
where possible. Person styling is secondary to flower presentation.

- APPROVE: Bouquet looks fresh and well-arranged. Fig & Bloom wrapping
  visible (white tissue, black ribbon, branded sticker) in most cases.
  Person styling is clean and appropriate — no dress code enforced.
  Genuine, candid emotion.
- COMMENT: Bouquet presentation is good but Fig & Bloom packaging not
  visible. Or bouquet looks slightly dishevelled (delivery transit?).
  Not disqualifying alone — flag for human call.
- REJECT: Flowers look wilted, damaged, or poorly arranged. Setting is
  inappropriate or clashes with brand tone. Competitive florist branding
  visible.

**Notes:**
- Person outfit does NOT matter. Athletic wear, formal dress, casual,
  pyjamas — all accepted if the bouquet looks good and the moment feels
  genuine.
- What matters is bouquet presentation and authenticity of the moment,
  not fashion styling.
- Stiff, overly posed shots are less on-brand than candid, natural
  moments — but are not grounds for rejection.

### 5. Technical Quality

**Standard:** Standard smartphone camera quality is the floor. No DSLR
or professional equipment required.

- APPROVE: Sharp enough to see flower detail. Adequate resolution for
  social media use. Intentional blur/bokeh is fine.
- COMMENT: Slightly soft focus on the bouquet (not the background).
  Minor motion blur. Low-light grain that doesn't obscure the flowers.
- REJECT: Severely blurry, pixelated, or low-resolution. Major motion
  blur. Image is corrupted or unreadable.

**Notes:**
- Intentional shallow depth of field (blurred background, sharp flowers)
  is a positive, not a flaw.
- Camera app UI visible in frame (grid lines, camera controls) is
  acceptable for behind-the-scenes content.

### 6. Brand Alignment (Overall Mood)

**Standard:** The image feels like a genuine moment of joy with flowers.
Warm, real, accessible. Not editorial, not overly polished, not stiff.

- APPROVE: The image tells a small story — someone received flowers and
  was moved to capture the moment. It feels authentic. It would sit
  comfortably in the Fig & Bloom Instagram feed alongside the brand's
  own photography and graphic tiles.
- COMMENT: The image is technically fine but feels generic or stock-like.
  Missing the warmth or personality that makes it feel like a real moment.
  Or the tone is slightly off-brand (too editorial, too casual, too
  provocative) but not disqualifying.
- REJECT: The image actively conflicts with brand tone — aggressive,
  dark, clinical, overly sexualised with flowers as an afterthought,
  or so heavily styled it feels inauthentic.

**Notes:**
- The brand's social feed alternates between photography, graphic quote
  tiles, and botanical shots. UGC slots into the photography lanes.
  When assessing brand alignment, imagine the image sitting in a 3-column
  grid next to a black-and-white quote graphic and a botanical close-up.
  Does it work?

---

## Hard-Reject Triggers

These override all other assessment. Any hit = immediate REJECT.

1. Visible competitor florist branding, logos, or packaging
2. Wrong product (not a Fig & Bloom bouquet, or product unidentifiable)
3. NSFW content, inappropriate imagery, or offensive material
4. Visible watermarks, stock photo marks, or attribution overlays
5. Image is corrupted, unreadable, or too low-resolution to assess
6. Flowers are clearly wilted, dead, or damaged

---

## Calibration Notes

- This rubric was calibrated from real Fig & Bloom UGC that the brand
  approved. The bar is deliberately inclusive — the brand values
  authenticity over polish.
- The most common reason to reject UGC for this brand is likely to be
  heavy filtering that distorts flower colours, or compositions where
  the bouquet is secondary.
- An agent using this rubric should expect a healthy approval rate (60-80%)
  for UGC from briefed influencer partnerships, and a lower rate (40-60%)
  for unsolicited customer UGC.
- When in doubt, verdict should be COMMENT, not APPROVE. False approves
  are worse than unnecessary human reviews.
