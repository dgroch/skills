# Fig & Bloom — Art Direction & Photography Guide

---

## Core Principle

> The model and the bouquet are co-heroes. Every image should feel like
> a fashion campaign that happens to feature flowers — not a florist
> who hired a model.

Fig & Bloom's photography borrows from fashion editorial rather than
product photography. The bouquet is never shot alone on a surface — it
lives in someone's hands, held with intention, styled with the same
care as the outfit. The aspiration is Viktoria & Woods meets premium
floristry.

---

## Lighting

**Primary:** Soft, even studio lighting with warm undertone. One primary
light source creating gentle directional quality — enough to give
dimension to the face and texture to the petals, but never harsh.
Colour temperature 3800–4500K.

**Fill:** Broad fill to keep shadows open on the opposite side. The
overall exposure should be bright and clean — the backdrop glows warmly,
skin reads beautifully, and flower colours pop at full saturation.

**Avoid:** Harsh directional shadows across the face. Cold/blue studio
light. Flat on-camera flash. Ring light catchlights. Dramatic chiaroscuro
— this brand is warm and welcoming, not moody.

---

## Backdrops

**Primary:** Light warm beige/parchment seamless paper or painted
backdrop, hex **#D8CCBE**. This is the brand's signature background —
it appears in the majority of editorial shots. The correct tone is a
quiet, neutral parchment — NOT terracotta, NOT rust, NOT orange-brown.
The flowers provide all the warmth and colour; the backdrop stays
understated so as not to compete. Always specify the hex value in
prompts: `light warm beige studio backdrop hex #D8CCBE, NOT terracotta,
NOT rust, NOT orange-brown`.

**Secondary:** Clean white or off-white for brighter seasonal content.
Soft grey for winter collections.

**Never:** Terracotta or rust backdrops (these read as a competitor
brand). Busy patterned backgrounds. Outdoor locations for editorial
shots (lifestyle is separate). Dark/moody backdrops. Concrete or
industrial textures.

---

## Composition

**Framing:** Three-quarter body (waist-up to above-knee) is the primary
frame. Full-body for full outfit display. Close-crop (shoulders-up with
bouquet) for detail and Stories content.

**Model position:** Slightly off-centre, body angled 15–30 degrees from
camera. The bouquet sits at hip-to-chest height, held naturally in both
hands or cradled in one arm.

**Bouquet presence:** The bouquet occupies at least 30% of the visible
frame. It should feel substantial and generous — Fig & Bloom bouquets
are large-scale, abundant, and dramatic.

**Negative space:** Enough clean backdrop visible to feel editorial and
allow the composition to breathe. The image should never feel crammed.

**Crop style:** Primary crops: 4:5 vertical (Instagram feed), 3:4
(product page), 9:16 (Stories). Always shoot wider than the tightest
intended crop.

---

## Model Direction

**Casting:** Women 25–40 as primary subjects. Diverse as default — not
as a checkbox. Natural beauty with minimal, polished makeup (skin-focus,
soft lip, groomed brow). Hair styled but not over-styled — loose waves,
clean bun, natural texture.

**Pose energy:** Contemplative, present, assured. The model holds the
bouquet like it belongs to her — not like she's been handed a prop.
Body language is relaxed but considered. Think editorial portraiture,
not catalogue.

**Gaze:** Model does NOT engage the camera. Looking to the side, down
at the bouquet, or away into middle distance. This creates the editorial
quality and avoids stock-photo energy.

**Hands:** Natural, relaxed grip. One hand supporting the bouquet from
beneath, the other resting against the wrapping or gently touching a
stem. Hands are critical — AI must render them cleanly.

---

## Model Styling

**Clothing:** Fashion-forward, seasonal. Styled to complement (not
compete with) the bouquet colours. Core wardrobe colours: noir black,
silk white, warm cream, blush, clay. Seasonal fashion pieces acceptable
when they harmonise — printed blouses, linen, knits.

**Collaboration approach:** Source styling from current-season fashion
brands (e.g. Viktoria & Woods autumn/winter range). The model's outfit
should look like she walked off a fashion editorial set and picked up
a bouquet on the way.

**Jewellery:** Minimal and quality. Gold hoops, a thin bracelet, a
single ring. Nothing that competes with the flowers.

**Shoes (full-body only):** Simple, elegant. Pointed-toe flats or low
heels in blush, nude, or black. Never sneakers, never heavy boots.

---

## Bouquet Styling

**Scale:** Large and abundant. Fig & Bloom bouquets are generous — they
should feel like a statement, not a gesture.

**Wrapping:** White tissue paper, visible. Black satin ribbon tied at
the stems. Fig & Bloom branded circle sticker on the tissue — visible
where natural, never forced.

**Flower palette:** Vivid and true-to-life. The flowers provide ALL the
colour in the image. Key varieties: phalaenopsis orchids (pink, fuchsia),
garden roses (blush, amber, peach), stock (purple, lilac), anthurium
(coral, salmon), gypsophila (pink-tinted or white), chrysanthemums
(cream, white), eucalyptus, snapdragons.

**Arrangement style:** Abundant but not chaotic. A clear colour story
within each bouquet — typically 3–4 hero flowers with supporting
textures. Orchids cascading forward is a signature compositional choice.

**Design fidelity (critical):** When generating from a named Fig & Bloom
design (Marseille, Portofino, Lucerne, Osaka, Broome, etc.), the image
MUST faithfully replicate that specific design — the correct flower
varieties, their proportions, and the colour story. A generic
"nice-looking bouquet" that does not match the named design is not
acceptable. The seed image drives fidelity in Mode B/C composites;
ensure the seed is correctly loaded and referenced. Always describe the
specific flower ingredients from the seed in the prompt (not generic
descriptors like "mixed flowers").

## Lifestyle Photography (Secondary Lane)

Not all content is editorial. The lifestyle lane shows flowers in
context — but still at a premium production level.

### Scene 1: The Tablescape
A styled dinner table with a Fig & Bloom arrangement as centrepiece.
Candlelight, linen napkins, quality tableware. Shot from eye level or
slightly above. Warm ambient light.

### Scene 2: The Styled Interior
An arrangement on a marble benchtop, console table, or bedside table.
Clean, aspirational interior. The space is beautiful, the flowers
complete it. No people.

### Scene 3: The Receiving Moment
A woman at her front door or in her hallway, bouquet just arrived. The
branded wrapping and ribbon visible. Genuine, not performative.

---

## Packaging Photography

**The bouquet IS the packaging.** White tissue, black ribbon, branded
sticker — these elements are integral to the product and should be
visible in the majority of editorial and product shots. The wrapping
is part of the brand experience, not something removed before
photographing.

---

## Always / Never

### Always
- Warm studio lighting for editorial (clay backdrop glows, skin reads warm)
- Vivid, saturated, true-to-life flower colours
- Fashion-forward model styling that complements the bouquet
- Model gaze away from camera — contemplative, present
- White tissue wrap and black satin ribbon visible
- Diverse casting as default
- Both model and bouquet in sharp focus
- Large, generous, abundant bouquets

### Never
- Model looking directly at camera (breaks editorial quality)
- Desaturated or muted flower colours (the vibrancy is the brand)
- Cool/blue lighting or colour grade
- Casual/athleisure model styling for editorial content
- Bouquet as afterthought or prop — it's a co-hero
- Busy backgrounds competing with the bouquet
- Stock photo energy — stiff, over-lit, generic
- Scattered petals, confetti, or styled mess
- Competitor packaging or generic wrapping

### Litmus Test
> Before publishing any image, ask: "Would this sit comfortably on
> a fashion brand's Instagram, between outfit posts and campaign shots?"
