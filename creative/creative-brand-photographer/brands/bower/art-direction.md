# Bower — Art Direction & Photography Guide

Extracted from: bower-art-direction.pdf

---

## Core Principle

> The arrangement is the product and the content. Every shot should make
> someone want to reach into the frame.

Product photography borrows from Rhode's "tactile minimalism" — close,
warm, textural, and never sterile.

---

## Lighting

**Primary:** Natural directional light. Warm side-light from a single
window source. Morning or late afternoon colour temperature (3500–4500K).
Creates soft, dimensional shadows.

**Fill:** Minimal. Use a single reflector (warm white or gold) at most.
No ring lights, no flat studio setups.

**Avoid:** Direct overhead fluorescent. Hard flash. Pure white studio
lighting. Anything that makes the image feel clinical.

---

## Composition

**Negative space:** 50–60% of frame. The arrangement should breathe.
Generous negative space in the brand's Linen tones (#EDE3D5) gives
the image editorial quality and creates room for text overlay.

**Depth of field:** Shallow — f/2.0–f/3.5 for hero shots. One focal
plane sharp (the front-most petals or a texture detail), background
falling to a soft warm blur.

**Crop style:** Primary crops: square (1:1 for grid), 4:5 vertical
(for feed and product pages), 16:9 horizontal (for hero banners
and email headers). Shoot for multiple crops simultaneously.

**Camera angle:** Eye-level or slightly below for hero shots (gives
arrangements presence and scale). Overhead at 45° for unboxing
and packaging moments.

---

## Props + Surfaces

**Surfaces:** Raw linen cloth (Linen palette tone). Warm stone or
terrazzo. Light timber with visible grain. Matte ceramic surfaces.

**Supporting props (max 2 per frame):** A single ceramic vase. A hand
or forearm reaching in. A coffee cup at the frame edge. A linen napkin.
A personal note card.

**Never in frame:** Plastic wrap or cellophane. Bright coloured props.
Generic glass vases. Confetti or scattered petals. Props that compete
for attention.

---

## Colour Grading

**Warm shift:** +10–15%. Push overall warmth gently toward the
Linen/Sandstone zone. Whites should read as warm cream, never blue-white.

**Greens:** Slightly desaturated. Pull green saturation down 10–15%
and shift toward sage/eucalyptus tones. This prevents foliage from
looking artificial or too vivid.

**Shadows:** Lifted, never crushed. Shadows should be open and warm —
lift blacks to around 10–15% so there's always detail and warmth,
even in the darkest areas.

**Contrast:** Gentle S-curve. Slight highlight compression, slight
shadow lift. The image should feel rich but never harsh.

---

## Lifestyle Scenes

### Scene 1: The Kitchen Table
Morning light. An arrangement sits on a kitchen table alongside the
everyday — a coffee cup, a book, a hand reaching to adjust a stem.
The mood is quiet morning contentment.

### Scene 2: The Doorstep Arrival
The gifting hero. A Bower box on a doorstep, or being received at
the door. The moment just before or just after opening.

### Scene 3: The Living Room, Day Three
An arrangement in its settled state — slightly more relaxed, petals
softened, in an established place in someone's real living room.
Not styled, just lived-in.

## Hero Editorial (website only)

Used exclusively for the homepage hero, which rotates weekly with the
current drop. This is a separate lane from product / lifestyle /
detail and does not appear on the Instagram grid.

**Editorial framing, not product photography.** The job here is to
sell a feeling, not to merchandise the bouquet. If a shot would work
as a PDP image, it's wrong for hero editorial.

**Arrangement is in the scene, not the subject of it.** The flowers
are an element within a moment — they're discovered by the viewer
rather than presented to them. The arrangement should feel
incidental, as if the photographer was shooting the room and the
flowers happened to be there.

**Context over isolation.** Place the flowers in a lived space — on a
table mid-meal, caught in a slice of window light, partially in
frame at the edge of a kitchen counter. Never on a clean studio
surface, never centred against negative space alone.

**Negative space and atmosphere prioritised.** The shot is mostly
mood. Wide framing, generous breathing room around the arrangement,
soft warm tones bleeding through the scene. Atmosphere reads first;
the arrangement reads second.

**Cropping that would be wrong for a PDP.** Tight, off-centre,
partial. The vase is half out of frame. A bloom is sliced by the
edge. The arrangement sits in the lower third with the entire upper
half of the image given to wall, light, and air. Choices that would
fail a product brief are exactly right here.

**Human traces but no humans.** The scene should imply someone has
just left or is about to return — a hand exiting the frame, a
half-poured glass, a folded linen napkin, a chair pushed back, a
book left open. No faces. No models. (See "Always / Never" — the
existing no-models rule still applies.)

**Aspect ratios:**
- **16:9** — primary (desktop hero)
- **4:5** — secondary (mobile crop)
- **Higgsfield mapping constraint:** 4:5 outputs render as 3:4 on
  the Higgsfield pipeline. When prompting for the 4:5 mobile crop
  via Higgsfield, frame for 3:4 and verify the safe area.

**Lighting and grade:** Same warm direction and grade as the rest of
the brand — natural sidelight, 3500–4500K, lifted shadows,
desaturated greens. The hero-editorial lane bends framing and
subject hierarchy, not the colour world.

## Casting Direction

**Who:** Women 25–40 as primary subjects. Diverse as default. Real
skin, natural nails, minimal jewellery (a thin gold ring is perfect).

**Styling:** Neutral, warm tones in clothing (linen, cream, soft knits).
No bold patterns or logos. The person is a supporting element, not the
subject.

**Energy:** Quiet, warm, genuine. Not performative joy. Soft smile,
a glance down at the flowers, the act of adjusting a stem.

**Avoid in casting:** Over-styled editorial models. Stiff posing.
Stock-photo energy.

---

## The Unboxing Sequence — 5 Key Moments

1. **The Box Arrives** — On a surface, doorstep or table. First impression.
2. **The Lid Lifts** — Hands opening the box, shot from slightly above.
3. **The Tissue Reveal** — Tissue paper parted, first glimpse of stems.
4. **First Stems Out** — Arrangement being lifted, controlled reveal.
5. **Placed in Vessel** — Final arrangement in its vase, first moment in context.

---

## Always / Never

### Always
- Warm natural light (morning or golden hour feel)
- Generous negative space (Linen tones)
- At least one human element per 4 product shots
- Consistent colour grade across all content (batch-grade)
- Shoot for multiple crops simultaneously
- Show the personal note card in gifting contexts
- Include the Bower box in ≥30% of lifestyle content

### Never
- White void backgrounds
- Over-styled flat-lays with scattered props
- Stock photo energy
- Cool or blue colour casts
- Filters or heavy presets that flatten detail
- Gratuitous arrangement destruction (scattered petals, wilting)
- Brand-logo watermarks on images
- Children or pets as primary subjects

### Litmus Test
> Before publishing any image, ask: "Would someone with great taste
> repost this without hesitation?"
